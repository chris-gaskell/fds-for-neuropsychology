Videos of Functional Seizures

Functional Dissociative Seizure
Disruption of usually integrated functions of consciousness, memory, identity, or perception (Goldstein et al., 2000).

Episodes which superficially resemble epileptic seizures or syncope, but which are not associated with the same physiological changes which underpin these conditions (Reuber, 2008).


  • One manifestation/constellation of the FND umbrella .

  • Functional referring to an impairment of normal functioning.

  • Lots of other terms to describe FDS (to discuss later).
  • Epilepsy but without the same underlying electrical processes. Different brain structural changes.
  • DSM-V-TR: Conversion disorder/Functional neurological symptom disorder.
  • Discrimination is essential for providing the most appropriate treatment.
  • Seizures are one manifestation, often within complex broader FND constellations.
  • FND is the umbrella term. If we see a diagnosis indicative of seizures then don’t forget the myriad of other symptoms which may be impacting the person’s ability to function.
  • This visualisation is taken from a large survey of people living with FND.
  • Cognitive and sensory symptom are very high.
  • 93% had fatigue!

Taken from a survey of 1048 individuals living with FND2

Who Makes The Diagnosis?

Usually made by a Neurologist or Neuropsychiatrist.

Assessments can include:

  • Clinical history.

  • Videos & subjective experience.

  • EEG (electroencephalogram).

  • ECG (electrocardiogram).

  • Video telemetry.

Any professional who potentially comes into contact with patients has a potential role in identification and/or clarification.

  • In order to provide understanding and appropriate treatments we need accurate and prompt diagnosis. The importance of sensitively delivered diagnosis can’t be overstated.

  • Establishing the diagnosis is challenging, particularly because of the need to carefully rule out epilepsy. it can require lots of assessments.

  • More assessments = more time. As the assessments are far from perfect it is common for FDS to be first misdiagnosed as epilepsy.

  • In order to increase the chance of an accurate diagnosis, it is important that the person is seen by someone who is particularly familiar seizure conditions.

  • Video-telemetry: inpatient admission so that the person can be observed and recorded having seizures.

  • Psychologists have a role in diagnosis too. Not making it, but often explaining it and helping the patient to make sense of it. Some people attend who report having not been told about the diagnosis.

Diagnostic Challenges

Difficult to distinguish from other conditions = long waits for diagnosis.

  • Long duration from onset to diagnosis4.

  • Functional seizures treated epileptic seizure protocols5.

  • Prolonged use of anti-convulsants.

  • Lack of access to necessary support and treatment.

Just to reinforce some of the dilemmas around diagnosis.

  • Journey to diagnosis can be very long. When using gold standard assessment (VT) mean duration of 8.4 years (median = 3) (Kerr et al., 2021)

  • Response to functional seizures with seizure protocols (very common, see Jungilligens et al., 2021).

  • Among 1210 patients presenting with prolonged seizures (status epilepticus) 8% went on to receive a diagnosis of FDS

How Common Is FDS?

Prevalence: Challenging to calculate6.

  • Norway: 24/100,0007 (95% CI = 18–30).
  • Subsequently estimated 50,000–100,000 people in the UK8.

Incidence:

  • Norway: 3.1/100,000 per year (95% CI = 2.4–3.7).

  • UK (older study): 4.09/100,0009.

Healthcare:

  • 8-12% of presentations to first seizure clinics10.

  • 11% of seizures presenting to emergency services11.

  • 20–30% of patients referred to epilepsy centers with intractable seizures12.

  • It’s difficult to accurately quantify prevalence rates.
  • Accurate data on the epidemiology of FDS are limited, with no UK population-level studies.
    • May feel more common from working in neuropsychology; however it is still a rare condition.
  • Estimated that FDS accounts for:
    • Approx 10% of people attending to a first seizure neurology clinic.
    • Approx 10% of seizures that present to A&E.
    • Approx a quarter of patients referred to epilepsy centers with intractable epilepsy.

Risk Factors

For a detailed account of risk and aetiological factors in FDS see Popkirov et al., (2019)13

Findings
Age

  • Often 2nd to 4th decades of life.

  • Young adults (15-19) most at risk14.

  • Age of onset:

    • Mean = 39.6 years (SD 16.1)15.

    • Mode = 16.
Sex & Gender

  • Female preponderance = 60-80%16 (ratio 3:1).

  • More even gender distribution for younger and older age of onset17.

  • Sexual and gender minorities18.
Demographics
  • Low SES and low rates of employment19.
Epilepsy
  • High co-morbidity rates20.
Learning Disabilities
  • Higher rates of FDS in individuals with an LD21.
Mental Health
  • High rates of anxiety (29%), depression (48%), & PTSD (23%)22.
Health:

  • Pain & Fatigue.

  • Sleep disturbance.

  • Migraine.

  • Traumatic Brain Injury23.

  • Neurosurgery24.

  • Neurodiversity25.

Lots of information out there about risk factors for FDS.

Take home point is that they don’t consistently discriminate. No risk factor can explain every presentation of FDS and there is broadly a consensus that the cause of FDS for many people is likely to be multi-factorial.

In short, FDS is more common in women around the ages of 20 to 40, although modal age of onset is 16, and there are elevated rates of FDS in:

  • LD.

  • Sexual and gender minorities.

  • People of low SES.

  • Epilepsy, brain injury, migraine.

  • Physical and mental health comorbidities.

  • And trauma.

Age:

  • Modal age of onset is 16. mean and median = 28.

  • Age disparity dissipates in late adulthood.

Epilepsy:

  • 12–22% Dual diagnosis (meta-analysis).

  • Challenging to diagnose

LD:

  • Median = 9.4% of comorbid ID was found across eleven PNES cohorts (Rawlings et al., 2021).

Demographics:

  • Over 50% of all patients live in areas categorised within the highest deprivation quintile

  • Over two- thirds of patients being unemployed at the point of seeking treatment for PNES (Goldstein et al., 2019).

Psychiatric:

  • PTSD: 38%

  • Norm rather than an exception (Nicholson et al., 2020).

  • Psychiatric conditions: especially affective, trauma-related and/or dissociative disorders, are typically present in over 50% of most FND samples, with lifetime rates even higher.

  • Personality disorders: higher rate than the general population. Particularly EUPD and cluster C presentations.

  • Epilepsy surgery: New onset following surgery (upto 9%).

  • TBI: Rates vary a lot. Pooled frequency of about 42%

Terminology and Language

Functional vs. Organic

  • Functional: Impairment of function.

  • Not suggesting a behavioural component26.

  • Functional-Organic terminology promotes dualism.

  • Can a person truly be non-organic?27

  • The ‘Functional vs. Organic’ dichotomy is quite a dominant discourse, particularly in neuropsychology, but it’s important to remind ourselves of the limitations of this distinction.

  • Functional vs organic fits the existing healthcare system quite well; but that is largely because our healthcare is heavily built on mind-body dualism; just look at the distinction between physical and mental health care. FND and FDS are conditions that illustrate very well how this distinction is certinaly not as clear cut as people think.

  • What organic symptoms are not in some way mediated by psychological processes? and if someone has limb weakness for many years, then could motor patterns not go on to influence brain structure through plasticity?

  • Functional, does not mean behavioural; it is an impairment of function.

  • Jon Stone makes an interesting point about the term organic being completely flawed, in an interesting paper called A tale of two turnips. I cannot do it justice, but maybe consider how anything in our very consciousness not be organic. Consciousness must arise from organic matter at some level. Structural.

ILAE28 have forwarded the position of adopting Functional Dissociative Seizure

The main reasons for changing are: 1) Almost everything in the universe is “nonepileptic”. 2) Patient organisations (FNDhope, FNDaction) want a different label. 3) International nosologies (DSM, ICD) use the terms “functional’ or “dissociative” seizures and it would be much better for all concerned if we could agree on a single term (although functional/dissociative seizures is clearly a compromise in this regard).

Labels Matter

The important point is that the words we use matter, and ultimately we can make suggestions to patients about which to use, but we should be guided by there preferences.

Treatment: Evidence & Measurement

Role of Psychology

  • Well suited to the treatment of FDS - but not suited for all.

  • Co-morbidity needs to be accounted for.

  • The paroxysmal role fairly well suited to psychological interventions (e.g., BT, CBT).

  • FND can respond; but may need MDT input.

  • MH should be treated irrespective of FND.

  • Some people with lots of health and MH comorbidities benefit from focused work on FND.

  • Some people need to work on the things underlying, which may be maintaining the FND.

  • For some people: If you spend time focusing on the smoke (core FND) but don’t work on the underlying distress maintaining then treatment might not be effective and patients may relapse.

The Evidence Base

Treatment Key papers
Psychological Therapy

  • Only a small amount of high quality evidence.

  • Seizure reduction and freedom29,30.

  • CODES - landmark trial31. Null findings for seizure frequency.

  • Other psychological improvements (HR-QoL, anxiety, depression, dissociation etc.)32.
Neuro-Stimulation
  • Emerging evidence base but yet to be confirmed33.
Medication
  • Lack of supportive evidence34
  • Lots of evidence for treatments functional seizures; although much of it is quite low quality, and mostly focused upon behavioral and CBT interventions.
  • Most focuses on seizure reduction, which is understandable because that is usually what people want to change when they arrive for treatment.
  • The problem however is that the evidence for psychological treatments for seizures frequency is mixed. Practice-based studies tend to show very impressive rates of seizure freedom (roughly 1 in 2) however the one high quality RCT for FDS found no difference to standard care.
  • So some been argued that psychological treatment may be less influential than we would hope for seizure frequency; however there are a lot of methodological problems in outcome research for FDS, least of which is how to accurately measure and count seizures, so it is quite hard to conclude anything definitively. That said, RCTs have found far more impressive findings for other outcomes, such as mental health, functioning, and quality of life, to name just a few.
  • Not enough time to go into detail, but there isn’t evidence supportive of medication, and there is some emerging evidence for neuro-modulation but it’s far too early to say how effective or suitable they are.

Acceptability

  • Some patients ambivalent to treatment.

  • Doesn’t always fit with patient view of difficulties.

  • Comes following a long journey.

  • High expectations.
